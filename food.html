Fish and Chips

Fish and chips are a beloved Kiwi classic and a staple of New Zealand's coastal culture. Introduced by British settlers in the 19th century, this dish quickly became a local favourite and is now a must-try for visitors wanting to experience authentic Kiwi comfort food. Typically, fresh fish like hoki, tarakihi, snapper, or gurnard is battered and deep-fried until golden and crispy, then served with thick-cut chips (fries). It's often wrapped in paper, perfect for enjoying at the beach, in a park, or as a casual takeaway meal. Popular condiments include tomato sauce, tartare sauce, or malt vinegar, and many locals love adding a side of pineapple fritters or battered sausages. What makes New Zealand fish and chips special is the freshness of the seafood—caught locally and cooked to order. Coastal towns and cities across the country boast their own iconic fish and chip shops, often passed down through generations. Whether you're eating it on the waterfront with seagulls nearby or grabbing a quick meal after aday of exploring, fish and chips offer a delicious, no-fuss taste of everyday life in New Zealand.

Meat Pie

The meat pie is a staple of New Zealand cuisine and a favourite among locals for its comforting, no-fuss appeal. Found in nearly every bakery, café, and dairy across the country, it's a go-to option for a quick, satisfying bite.Traditionally, a Kiwi meat pie features tender minced or chopped meat—commonly beef—encased in a buttery, flaky pastry shell. It's often paired with rich gravy and may include tasty extras like cheese, mushrooms, or onions. Popular flavours range from mince and cheese to steak and pepper, and even more creative fillings like Thai curry or vegetarian lentil. What sets the New Zealand version apart is the emphasis on handheld convenience and bold, savoury flavours. They're made to be eaten anywhere—on a park bench, during a road trip, or straight out of a paper bag at the beach. For visitors, grabbing a hot meat pie is a delicious way to experience everyday Kiwi life. Whether from an award-winning bakery or a humble corner shop, the meat pie remains a much-loved part of New Zealand's food identity.

Pavlova

Pavlova is a classic New Zealand dessert and a proud part of the country's culinary heritage. Light, elegant, and delicious, it's especially popular during summer and festive occasions like Christmas.Named after Russian ballerina Anna Pavlova, the dessert features a crisp meringue shell with a soft, marshmallow-like center. It's typically topped with freshly whipped cream and a generous serving of seasonal fruit—most commonly kiwifruit, strawberries, and passionfruit. While there's an ongoing friendly rivalry with Australia over who created it first, pavlova holds a special place in Kiwi hearts. Its combination of textures—crunchy on the outside, fluffy on the inside—makes it a crowd-pleaser at any gathering. For tourists, pavlova is a must-try if you're after a sweet taste of New Zealand. Whether enjoyed in a café, at a family barbecue, or homemade by locals, it perfectly captures the light and fresh flavours that define New Zealand's food culture.

Hokey Pokey Ice Cream

Hokey pokey ice cream is one of New Zealand's most iconic and beloved desserts. This classic Kiwi flavour combines smooth, creamy vanilla ice cream with crunchy nuggets of honeycomb toffee, known as hokey pokey. The contrast between the soft ice cream and the golden, crackly toffee creates a texture and taste that's both nostalgic and uniquely New Zealand. First appearing in the mid-20th century, hokey pokey ice cream quickly became a national favourite. Today, it's a staple in nearly every dairy, supermarket, and ice cream shop across the country. Many New Zealanders grow up eating it, and it's often the first flavour they recommend to visitors. The toffee pieces are made from a simple mix of sugar, golden syrup, and baking soda, which puffs up to create the airy, caramel-like crunch. Though the ingredients are humble, the result is a flavour that's rich, sweet, and distinctly Kiwi. Whether you're enjoying a cone by the beach, grabbing a tub for a family barbecue, or trying it for the first time, hokey pokey ice cream is a must-try that captures the fun, laid-back spirit of New Zealand.
